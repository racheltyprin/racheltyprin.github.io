BRAIN CONNECTIVITY RESEARCH

Mapping the relationship between the brain's physical wiring and its activity patterns,
with implications for how we model and understand neurological health.

———

The Context

A fundamental question in computational neuroscience is whether you can predict how regions of the brain communicate functionally just by knowing how they are physically connected. A 2023 paper tackled this directly, modeling communication through the brain's structural connectome1Structural connectome — A map of the physical white matter connections between brain regions, derived from diffusion MRI. to generate simulated functional connectivity2Functional connectivity — The degree to which brain regions show synchronized activity over time, measured from fMRI scans.. Their best result came from subnet communicability3Subnet communicability — A measure of how easily information spreads through the brain, calculated only through a small subset of backbone nodes rather than the full network.: a communication model that routes through a small set of backbone nodes4Backbone nodes — Brain regions chosen to act as communication relays for subnet communicability., rather than the full brain network, with the idea that a few well-placed hubs can capture the brain's overall communication patterns. Subnet communicability achieved a mean Pearson r5Pearson r — A correlation coefficient between -1 and 1; higher means better prediction. of 0.371. I set out to replicate their methodology and see if I could find a better one.

The Approach

I replicated the 2023 paper’s methodology using the same 1000Brains dataset, but my results didn’t support their central claim that subnet communicability outperformed communicability. I found that full-network communicability6Full-network communicability — A measure of how easily information spreads between all pairs of brain regions simultaneously, using the full structural connectome rather than routing through a subset of nodes. outperformed every subnet communicability variant I tested. Rather than stopping at a contradictory finding, I surveyed more recent literature and implemented several communication models the paper hadn’t tested including heat kernel diffusion7Heat kernel diffusion — Simulates how a signal spreads and decays through a network over time., cosine communicability8Cosine communicability — Measures how similar two brain regions’ overall communication patterns are across the whole network..

The Results

Two new models meaningfully outperformed the paper's best result: heat kernel diffusion at its optimal setting achieved a mean r of 0.393 and cosine communicability reached 0.392, both roughly a 6% improvement over the paper's 0.371 for subnet communicability. Their subnet variants also cleared that bar (subnet cosine communicability peaked at r=0.380 and subnet heat kernel at r=0.375), though neither matched their own full-network counterparts. The original paper's methodology was a reasonable starting point, but these findings suggest that the brain's communication patterns are better captured by models that account for how signals spread and decay over time.
