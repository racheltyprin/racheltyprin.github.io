SF BAY AREA MICROCLIMATE PREDICTION

A three-phase ML pipeline that learns the physical and atmospheric drivers of Bay Area microclimates to generate neighborhood-level weather predictions across the region.

———

The Context

The Bay Area has incredibly hyperlocal weather. On any given afternoon, the Sunset District might be fogged in and 57°F while the Mission, two miles away, is sunny and 72°F. Scale that dynamically across the full Bay Area region, and it gets even more complex: San Francisco and San Jose can feel like different climates entirely. Growing up here, I noticed weather forecasts were inaccurate, and couldn’t find a tool that actually captured the granularity of the weather. Coarse atmospheric reanalysis data covers the Bay Area with just 42 grid points across 7,000 square miles, and while ground station networks exist, they are unevenly distributed and designed for aviation and agriculture rather than neighborhood-level prediction. What's missing is a model that learns why neighborhoods behave differently, because when the forecast is wrong, it's not just an inconvenience, it shapes how people move through their day.

The Approach

The pipeline is designed in three stages. Stage 2 adds an LSTM5LSTM — A recurrent neural network architecture designed to learn patterns across time sequences. for temperature and humidity. Stage 3 predicts at arbitrary grid points across the Bay Area, producing a continuous weather map from which microclimate zones emerge based on learned behavior rather than geography. The architecture was a deliberate rejection of the published Geo-LSTM-Kriging approach, which has no way to represent vertical atmospheric structure and couldn't achieve the sub-degree accuracy needed to differentiate Bay Area neighborhoods. Data engineering was equally consequential: quality control across 1,124 stations surfaced three mechanistically distinct failure modes, each requiring different treatment, and a key derived feature encoding each station's position relative to the marine layer boundary meaningfully improved performance near the fog edge.

The Results

Stage 1 is complete. The gradient-boosted models trained on 582 quality-controlled stations predict temperature to within 0.7°C and humidity to within 4.3%, reducing error by roughly 70% compared to using coarse atmospheric data directly. A second model version added features encoding each station's position relative to the marine layer boundary, meaningfully improving performance for stations near the fog edge. Feature engineering improvements are ongoing before moving to Stage 2.
